About Public Relief

In a society where many things feel uncertain, we wanted to be the difference. While headlines shift daily and trust in institutions continues to erode, one thing should always remain reliable: that your help truly helps. That’s why we started Public Relief — an independent, transparent donation platform built on one simple idea: people deserve to know where their money goes. Every time someone donates through Public Relief, their contribution is tracked from start to finish. Whether it’s going toward emergency food supplies in drought-stricken regions, rebuilding schools after natural disasters, or supporting mental health services for war survivors, we document and report every step of the journey. You’ll get a personal impact report with photos, GPS coordinates, receipts, and testimonials from people on the ground. That’s what we mean by radical transparency. We partner only with vetted, community-led organizations — teams that live and work in the regions they serve. 87% of our partner organizations are locally run, and 94% of our project funding goes directly to operational aid. We don’t believe in “administrative black holes” where donations vanish into overhead — in fact, our operational costs are covered by private sponsors, so 100% of your donation goes to the cause. Our platform also features a Live Impact Dashboard, where you can follow real-time updates from the field. See how many water pumps have been installed this week in Malawi. Read how a micro-loan changed Fatima’s life in Morocco. Watch a short video from a community kitchen in Rio de Janeiro that you helped fund. We’re not just a middleman — we’re a movement. A movement for radical trust, for clarity, for people-powered change. Since 2021, over 132,000 individuals have joined our mission, funding more than 2,800 projects across 38 countries. That’s over 1.2 million lives touched — and counting. We are Public Relief. Transparent. Accountable. Human. Because when the world feels uncertain, certainty in your compassion shouldn't be a luxury — it should be the standard.
